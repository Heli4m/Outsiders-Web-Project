Psychological and Physical Effects of Gang Violence on Adolsecents

A Website By: Kevin, Cherry, Bin, Bim and Liam

Scars of Tulsa

When we read the Outsiders, we generally think of a rumble as a physical clash between two rival groups. However, the true damage of gang violence isn't found in the bruises, but found in the psychological restructuring of a child's mind.

In the mid 1960s, author S.E.Hinton explored a cycle of trauma surrounding the issues of gang violence, especially on a group of teenagers/young adults. Through various different characters, the author does a great job at explaining and showcasing the horrors of gang violence and what it can do to an adolescent.

From Fiction to Reality

This website connects the fictional experiences of our beloved Greasers to modern day articles and socio-psychological research, with the purpose of showing you the fact that the problem is still rampant to this day. By comparing a work of literature in the 1960s to studies nowadays, we have uncovered a chilling truth: even though the weapons in gang violence have changed and evolved, the "fight or flight" instincts and the psychological changes remain the same within teenagers.

Johnny Cade is a 16 year old boy, although often mistaken for younger due to his small stature. Unfortunately, during a fight with Darrel Curtis, Ponyboy Curtis ran away from home and brought Johnny with him, leading to a series of events that eventually leads to him committing manslaughter, getting hospitalized and passing away.

Johnny Cade clearly displays a case of PTSD due to his abusive upbringing and his near-fatal beating by the Socials. Due to this, he is potrayed by Ponyboy Curtis as perpetually "jumpy" and insecure, pushing him towards carrying a switchblade in order to protect himself, despite never having done so beforehand. CONFIDENTIAL // CASE-02

SUBJECT: WINSTON, DALLAS

Clinical Quote: "You get hardened in jail. I don't want that to happen to you. Like it happened to me..."

Status: Deceased - Police involved shooting

Physical Description: Elvish face, high cheekbones, pointed teeth, blonde hair

Dallas Winston is a 17 year old textbook juvenile delinquent who was jailed at the young age of 10 due to his involvement with New York street gangs. Similar to Johnny, Dallas also grew up in a neglectful home, which resulted in his chaotic experiences during his youth. As he grew, Dallas Winston continued to get into trouble for various crimes, including but not limited to stealing, fighting and drunkeness. Unfortunately, unable to bear the death of Johnny Cade, Dally recklessly stole from a store and pointed his firearm at the police, causing them to shoot him out of fear, leading to his passing.

Psychoanalysis: Emotional Desensitzation

Being exposed to violence and jail time at such a young age, his prefrontal cortex engaged a defensive mechanism known as "affective blunting", where he affectively turned off his ability to feel empathy or fear. Dallas is aware of this himself, even stating that the system changed him and made him "tough", which even he acknowledges that it is horrible.

CONFIDENTIAL // CASE-03

SUBJECT: CURTIS, DARREL

Clinical Quote: "Darry's gone through enough in his twenty years to be a hundred."

Status: Alive

Physical Description: 6'2", broad-shouldered, muscular, pale blue eyes

Darrel Curtis is a 20 year old young adult that is the current and sole guardian of two of his younger brothers. Described to be stern and cold by Ponyboy, he has to work two jobs in order to take care of his siblings after his parents passed away due to a car crash. Prior to his parents' passing, Darry was an excellent student and a great athelete, but he was forced to abandon his dreams due to the burden of stepping up as a parent and raising his siblings.

Psychoanalysis: Parentification

Darrel Curtis is a prime example of parentification, a phenomena where a child or a young adult is forced to step up to the role of a parent and must hold a grown adult's responsibility. Darrel's pale blue-green ice eyes and constant temper is a result of high and permanent cortisol levels, caused by the emotional strain and physical effort he needs to take care of his younger siblings.

Conclusion

Overall, the evidence seen through the characters Johnny Cade, Dallas Winston and Darrel Curtis reveals a singular truth: gang violence is not just a physical or a social conflict, but rather a neurological problem as well. While the rumbles in 1960's Tulsa left temporary bruises on these adolescents' physical body, the psychological problem on these young men have created shifts in their personal development.

The Issue With the Boys

Our cross analysis with the Outsiders against recent time's socio-psychological data confirms distinct psychological changes among our Greasers.

  • Hypervigilance/PTSD (Johnny Cade): When the environment becomes so threatening that an individual's brain stays in a higher state of alarm, leading to anxiety and "jumpiness".
  • Emotional Blunting (Dallas Winston): When early violence and neglect forces the prefrontal cortex to shut down strong emotions as a survival mechanism.
  • Parentification (Darrel Curtis): When the burden of taking care and the stress for providing in a violent environment leads to premature maturization and stress.

Closing Statement

It doesn't matter if it is a switchblade in a park in 1965 or the stressing system in 2026, the primitive methods of the human brain still function the exact same way. The Greasers wasn't just a normal street gang, it was a trauma bonded support system built around a group of boys trying to survive in a world that already have neglected them. As our studies show, the problem with these boys aren't just relics of the past, but the psychological and mental realities of adolescents living, to this day.
